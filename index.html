Professional Introduction

My name is Shiel Loganadan, and I am completing my Master’s in Parks, Recreation, Tourism and Sport Management (MPRTSM). I am interested in pursuing a career in sport and recreation facility operations, event management, and planning roles where safety, logistics, participant experience, and organizational priorities all have to work together.

When I entered the program, I had a general sense of how sport and recreation organizations function, but I was still thinking about many of those environments from the outside. I could usually recognize when a facility felt organized or when an event seemed well run, but I did not fully understand how many decisions, trade-offs, and behind-the-scenes systems made that possible. One of the most important changes in my thinking has been learning to approach these settings less as an observer and more as someone responsible for the outcome.

That shift happened gradually through assignments that demanded more than explanation. In projects involving facility planning, benchmarking, public engagement, destination development, event design, and organizational diagnosis, I had to decide what mattered most, what evidence deserved emphasis, and what kinds of recommendations were actually realistic. Over time, I became more aware that strong professional work depends not just on having information, but on interpreting it clearly, prioritizing it thoughtfully, and presenting it in a way that supports action.

Another major area of growth for me was how I came to understand experience. Earlier in the program, I mostly thought about participant or guest experience in visible terms such as service, enjoyment, or atmosphere. I now think much more about the operational choices that shape those experiences long before they are visible to the public, including staffing, timing, sequencing, access, communication, and preparedness. That perspective changed the way I approached my work because it made me pay more attention to the systems that support a smooth, safe, and well-run experience.

The strongest experiences in sport, recreation, and tourism are often shaped by decisions most people never notice directly: planning, coordination, access, communication, and operational follow-through.

Program & Facility Planning

Facility design, access equity, and operational application
Why this matters: Shows my ability to use LOS and access analysis to turn a broad service gap into focused, realistic facility recommendations.

Context: This artifact focused on identifying a major service gap in the City of Naples park system and recommending improvements tied to level-of-service measures, parity, and operational capacity.

My Role: I completed the planning analysis independently, interpreted the access and LOS data, and developed the recommendations and reflection.

Skills Demonstrated: Facility planning, LOS interpretation, equity analysis, operational thinking, and recommendation writing.

Reflection: This project stands out to me because it was one of the first times I felt myself thinking less like a student answering a prompt and more like someone trying to solve a real service problem. It was not enough to point out that a gap existed. I had to decide what kind of response would actually make sense in a constrained municipal setting. Instead of relying on ideal solutions, I focused on operationally meaningful recommendations such as a neighborhood park node, shared-use agreements, and expanded hours at River Park. That process helped me understand that good planning is often less about coming up with the biggest idea and more about identifying the change that is both meaningful and realistic.

Final Paper – City of Naples Parks & Recreation Department

Diagnosing and Reframing a City Parks & Recreation Department

Organizational diagnosis, leadership frames, and strategic redesign
Why this matters: Demonstrates my ability to analyze organizational problems from multiple perspectives rather than jumping to a single-cause explanation.

Context: This paper analyzed the City of Naples Parks & Recreation Department through organizational behavior and leadership frameworks, focusing on strategy-execution gaps and mission drift.

My Role: I conducted the diagnosis, applied Bolman and Deal’s four frames, and proposed a four-part solution tied to execution, workforce, and mission clarity.

Reflection: This project changed the way I think about organizational problems because it showed me how easy it is to oversimplify them. What I found most valuable was realizing that visible operational issues often point to deeper alignment problems. That made me more careful about how I diagnose systems and more interested in solutions that address root causes rather than only symptoms.

Final Reflection

Looking across this portfolio, the biggest change I see is in how I now approach a problem. Earlier in the program, I was more focused on understanding concepts thoroughly and making sure I could explain them clearly. As I moved through the coursework, I became much more interested in what should actually be done, what trade-offs were realistic, and how a recommendation would function once it left the page. That shift changed the way I approached nearly every major assignment.

A lot of that growth came from work that required decisions rather than summary. In projects involving facility planning, benchmarking, public engagement, organizational diagnosis, tourism development, and event design, I was not just repeating course ideas. I had to decide what deserved emphasis, which audiences were being underserved, what constraints would matter most, and how different choices would affect people in practice. That made me more aware that judgment is often what separates complete work from genuinely useful work.

Another important development for me was recognizing how deeply operations shape experience. Before this program, I mostly thought about experience in visible terms such as service, atmosphere, or enjoyment. What I understand more clearly now is that many of the things people value most are created by systems they never directly see, including preparation, staffing, access, communication, scheduling, and risk awareness. That realization changed the way I think about both facilities and events because it made me more attentive to the decisions that quietly determine whether an experience feels smooth, safe, and well organized.

I also became more thoughtful about the relationship between planning and public value. My work on Naples and on tourism development pushed me to think more carefully about access, equity, participation, and who benefits from the recommendations being made. Rather than staying at the level of general statements, I became more interested in measurable gaps, spatial patterns, and the question of whether a system is serving people fairly and intentionally. That perspective made my work feel more grounded and more connected to real communities.

Putting this portfolio together helped me see my own development more clearly. The strongest artifacts were not necessarily the longest or most complex assignments, but the ones where I could feel a real change in how I was thinking. They were the projects where I had to weigh competing priorities, make practical judgments, and present information in a way that another person could actually use. In that sense, this portfolio represents more than completed coursework. It shows a progression in how I learned to think, decide, and communicate within the kinds of professional settings I hope to enter.

Overall, I believe this work reflects growth from learning concepts in class to applying them in a more practical, analytical, and professionally aware way. I still see areas where I can continue developing, especially through more direct professional experience, but I also think this portfolio shows that I am much better prepared than I was at the start of the program to contribute in environments where operations, planning, communication, and participant experience all need to work together.

What this portfolio represents:

More than anything, this portfolio reflects growth in judgment. The most important change was not simply learning more content, but becoming better at making decisions, identifying what matters most, and presenting work in a way that supports real use.
