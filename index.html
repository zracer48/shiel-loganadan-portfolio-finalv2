Professional Introduction

My name is Shiel Loganadan, and I am completing my Master’s in Parks, Recreation, Tourism and Sport Management (MPRTSM). I am interested in pursuing a career in sport and recreation facility operations, event management, and broader planning roles where safety, logistics, participant experience, and organizational priorities all have to work together.

When I entered the program, I had a general understanding of how sport and recreation organizations function, but most of that understanding came from an outside perspective. I could usually tell when a facility seemed organized or when an event felt well run, but I did not fully understand how much planning, communication, analysis, and coordination were happening behind the scenes to create that result. One of the biggest changes for me throughout the program has been learning to think less like an observer and more like someone who is actually responsible for the outcome.

Across my coursework, I was repeatedly pushed beyond description. I had to decide where resources should go, which audiences were being underserved, how a plan should be framed for decision-makers, and what trade-offs were realistic in a given setting. That changed the way I approached my work because I began paying more attention to the operational decisions that support a smooth, safe, and well-organized experience.

One of the clearest lessons I took from this program is that strong participant and guest experiences are usually built by decisions that happen long before the public arrives: planning, staffing, sequencing, communication, access, and risk management.
Planning with evidence I became much more comfortable grounding recommendations in inventory data, LOS benchmarks, stakeholder analysis, and implementation constraints.

PRT 595 Program & Facility Planning

Program & Facility Planning

Facility design, access equity, and operational application

Context: This artifact focused on identifying a major service gap in the City of Naples park system and recommending realistic improvements tied to level-of-service measures, parity, and operational capacity.

My Role: I completed the planning analysis independently, interpreted the access and LOS data, and developed the recommendations and reflection.

Skills Demonstrated: Facility planning, operational thinking, LOS interpretation, equity analysis, and recommendation writing.

Reflection: This project forced me to move from simply noticing that some parks seemed better located or better equipped than others to proving that imbalance with measurable indicators. The most important decision I had to make was not just where the problem existed, but which interventions were actually realistic in a constrained city system. Instead of defaulting to expensive new construction, I focused on smaller but operationally meaningful choices like a neighborhood park node, shared-use agreements, and expanded hours at River Park. That made me think more like a manager working within limits rather than a student proposing ideal solutions with no implementation pressure. It also helped me see how access, scheduling, and facility planning are connected much more tightly than I originally assumed.

Final Reflection

Looking back across the work in this program, one of the biggest changes I see is in how I approach a problem. Earlier on, I was more focused on understanding concepts and making sure I could explain them clearly. As I moved through the program, I started thinking less about simply describing how an organization, event, or facility works and more about what I would actually do if I were responsible for the outcome. That shift may sound subtle, but for me it changed the way I approached almost every major assignment.

A lot of that development came from assignments that required decisions rather than summaries. In projects involving planning, benchmarking, event development, public engagement, tourism analysis, and organizational diagnosis, I was not just repeating course concepts. I had to decide what mattered most, which audiences were being underserved, what kinds of recommendations were realistic, and how different choices would affect participants, staff, partners, and communities. That process helped me understand that professional judgment often matters more than simply having a lot of information.

One clear pattern across these artifacts is that my thinking became more implementation-oriented over time. In earlier work, I tended to focus on whether an idea was strong in theory. In later work, I paid more attention to whether it could actually be carried out by real people under real constraints. That included budget limits, staffing shortages, political visibility, accessibility needs, timing, and communication clarity. I now understand much more clearly that a plan is only useful if the people receiving it can interpret it, trust it, and act on it.

Another important area of growth for me was recognizing the relationship between operations and experience. Before this program, I mostly thought of experience in visible terms, such as atmosphere, service, or enjoyment. What I understand now is that many of the things people value most are actually produced by systems they never see directly, including planning decisions, staffing preparation, communication procedures, scheduling, accessibility choices, and risk management. A smooth experience is often the result of effective operational choices made long before the participant arrives.

My work on Naples in particular helped me become more analytical about equity, access, and public value. I moved from making general claims about fairness or service quality to identifying measurable gaps, locating them spatially, and connecting them to groups of people who would feel those effects most directly. That was an important shift because it made my planning work more grounded. It also made me more aware that professional recommendations gain strength when they can show who benefits, how improvement would be measured, and why the recommendation fits the actual setting.

The tourism and microentrepreneurship artifacts added a different but equally important layer to my growth. They made me think more carefully about the relationship between experience design, local participation, and economic structure. I learned that strong destinations are not just those that attract people. They are also those that create meaningful pathways for local businesses and communities to participate in and benefit from that success. That perspective expanded how I think about events and tourism experiences, because I now pay more attention to whose stories are being centered, who captures value, and how partnerships can be structured more intentionally.

Putting this portfolio together also made something else clear to me: completing work and presenting work are not the same thing. At first, I approached the portfolio too much like an archive of assignments. Revising it pushed me to think more intentionally about how the artifacts connect and what they collectively say about my professional growth. That process helped me see the portfolio less as a checklist and more as a narrative about how my thinking developed throughout the program.

Overall, this portfolio reflects more than completed coursework. It reflects how my thinking has changed during the MPRTSM program, especially in learning how to evaluate operational problems, build evidence-based recommendations, communicate more strategically, and connect planning to real-world outcomes. I still see areas where I can continue improving, but I also think this work shows a clear progression from learning concepts in class to beginning to apply them in a more practical, analytical, and professional way.

More than anything, the work collected here shows that my growth was not just in writing better papers. It was in learning how to make clearer decisions, ask better questions, justify trade-offs, and present information in a way that another person could actually use.
