Professional Introduction

My name is Shiel Loganadan, and I am completing my Master’s in Parks, Recreation, Tourism and Sport Management (MPRTSM). I am interested in pursuing a career in sport and recreation facility operations and event management, especially in settings where planning, safety, and guest experience all need to work together at a high level.

When I entered the program, I had a general understanding of how sport and recreation organizations function, but most of that understanding came from an outside perspective. I could usually tell when a facility seemed organized or when an event felt well run, but I did not fully understand how much planning, communication, and coordination were happening behind the scenes to create that result. One of the biggest changes for me throughout the program has been learning to think less like an observer and more like someone who is actually responsible for the outcome.

Across my coursework, I was consistently asked to do more than summarize ideas from class. I had to make choices, justify those choices, and think through the trade-offs involved in different approaches. In assignments related to facility planning, booking policies, emergency action planning, and event risk assessment, I had to decide what should be prioritized, what compromises were reasonable, and what a manager would have to consider in practice. Those decisions were not always straightforward. In many cases, I had to weigh efficiency against safety, revenue against access, or detail against usability. Working through those trade-offs made the work feel more real and helped me better understand the responsibilities involved in managing sport and recreation environments.

Another major shift in my thinking was how I came to understand guest experience. Earlier in the program, I mostly thought about experience in terms of what participants or attendees directly see, such as service, atmosphere, or enjoyment. Over time, I started to recognize that the overall quality of an experience is often shaped by things the guest never notices, including staffing structure, communication systems, emergency procedures, crowd flow, and preparation before the event even begins. That changed the way I approached my work because I began paying more attention to the operational decisions that support a smooth, safe, and well-organized experience.

One of the clearest lessons I took from this program is that a strong guest experience is often built by decisions the guest never sees directly: planning, staffing, communication, preparation, and risk management.

Final Reflection

Looking back across the work in this program, one of the biggest changes I see is in how I approach a problem. Earlier on, I was more focused on understanding concepts and making sure I could explain them clearly. As I moved through the program, I started thinking less about simply describing how an organization or facility works and more about what I would actually do if I were responsible for the outcome. That shift may sound subtle, but for me it changed the way I approached almost every major assignment.

A lot of that development came from assignments that required decisions rather than summaries. In projects involving planning, benchmarking, event development, and organizational analysis, I was not just repeating course concepts. I had to decide what mattered most, what trade-offs were acceptable, and how different choices could affect participants, staff, and the organization itself. That process helped me understand that management decisions are rarely as simple as they appear from the outside. In many cases, there is no perfect answer. There is only a decision that has to be justified based on context, priorities, and what is realistic to implement.

Another important area of growth for me was recognizing the difference between writing a document and creating something that could actually function in practice. Earlier in the program, I often thought that if a plan was detailed and complete, then it was strong. Over time, I came to see that usefulness matters more than length. A plan has to be clear, realistic, and usable by the people who would carry it out. That became especially obvious in assignments involving facility planning, executive communication, and planning recommendations, where the value of the work depended not just on whether it was thorough, but on whether it could realistically guide action.

I also developed a stronger understanding of how deeply operations shape the participant or guest experience. Before this program, I mostly thought of experience in visible terms, such as customer service, atmosphere, or enjoyment. What I understand now is that many of the things people value most are actually produced by systems they never see, including planning decisions, staffing preparation, communication procedures, scheduling, and risk management. A smooth experience is often the result of effective operational choices made long before the participant arrives.

Putting this portfolio together also made something else clear to me: completing work and presenting work are not the same thing. At first, I approached the portfolio too much like an archive of assignments. Revising it pushed me to think more intentionally about how the artifacts connect and what they say about my professional growth. That process helped me see the portfolio less as a checklist and more as a narrative about how my thinking developed throughout the program.

Overall, this portfolio reflects more than completed coursework. It reflects how my thinking has changed during the MPRTSM program, especially in learning how to evaluate operational problems, make more intentional decisions, and connect planning to real-world outcomes. I still see areas where I can continue improving, but I also think this work shows a clear progression from learning concepts in class to beginning to apply them in a more practical and professional way.

Key Growth Areas

  • Thinking more like a decision-maker instead of only a student
  • Understanding trade-offs in real operational settings
  • Recognizing that usability matters more than just detail
  • Connecting behind-the-scenes planning to guest experience
