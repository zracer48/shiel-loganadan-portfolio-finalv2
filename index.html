Professional Introduction

My name is Shiel Loganadan, and I am currently completing my Master’s in Parks, Recreation, Tourism and Sport Management (MPRTSM). I am interested in pursuing a career in sport and recreation facility operations and event management, particularly in environments where operations, safety, and guest experience all have to work together at a high level.

Coming into this program, I had a general understanding of how sport and recreation organizations function, but most of that understanding was from the outside looking in. I could usually tell when something seemed organized, efficient, or well run, but I did not fully understand how much planning and coordination it took behind the scenes to create that result. One of the biggest changes for me throughout the program has been learning to think less like an observer and more like someone who is actually responsible for those decisions.

Across my coursework, I was often put in situations where I had to do more than explain ideas from class. I had to make choices, defend those choices, and think through the consequences of different approaches. In assignments related to facility planning, booking policies, emergency action planning, and event risk, I had to decide what should be prioritized, what trade-offs were reasonable, and what a manager would need to consider in practice. Those choices were not always simple. In many cases, I had to weigh efficiency against safety, revenue against access, or detail against usability. Working through those kinds of decisions made the material feel much more real and also helped me better understand the responsibilities that come with managing sport and recreation settings.

Another major shift in my thinking was how I came to understand guest experience. Earlier in the program, I tended to think about experience mostly in terms of what a participant or attendee directly sees. Over time, I started to recognize that the quality of an experience is often shaped by things the guest never notices, such as staffing structure, communication systems, crowd flow, emergency procedures, and preparation before the event even begins. That realization changed the way I approached assignments because I started paying more attention to the hidden operational decisions that support a smooth, safe, and enjoyable experience.

This portfolio reflects that development. Rather than treating it as just a collection of assignments, I see it as a record of how my thinking has changed during the program. The artifacts show not only the types of work I completed, but also how I started to take more ownership over my decisions, how I approached different problems, and how I began connecting academic assignments to the realities of professional practice in sport and recreation management.

Facility and Venue Operations

SEVM Facility Planning Project

Facility Planning Project

Context: A comprehensive analysis of Allegiant Stadium examining venue design, operational considerations, and the strategic role of a multipurpose stadium in a tourism-driven market.

My Role: I independently researched, analyzed, and developed the written facility planning project.

Skills Demonstrated: Facility planning, venue operations analysis, tourism market understanding, strategic facility evaluation.

Reflection Draft: When I first started this project, I approached it mostly as a research assignment and focused on gathering information about Allegiant Stadium. As I worked further into it, I realized that simply describing the venue was not enough. I had to start asking why certain design and operational decisions were made and what impact those choices would actually have on the way the stadium functions. One of the biggest challenges was connecting physical features to operational outcomes. For example, I initially saw entry points, seating layout, and circulation areas as design details, but through the project I started viewing them as factors that directly influence crowd flow, staffing needs, guest access, and even emergency response. I also made a deliberate decision to focus on the stadium as a multipurpose venue in a tourism-heavy market rather than only as a football facility. That changed the entire lens of my analysis because flexibility, scheduling, and venue adaptability became just as important as sports-specific design. This project helped me move beyond surface-level description and start thinking about facilities as operating systems that have to support safety, efficiency, and guest experience all at the same time.

Risk Management / Venue Safety Coursework

Facility Emergency Action Plan

Context: Development of an emergency response framework outlining staff roles, communication procedures, evacuation planning, and safety coordination in a large stadium environment.

My Role: I created the emergency action plan based on best practices and course guidance.

Skills Demonstrated: Risk management, emergency preparedness, communication planning, venue safety protocol design.

Reflection Draft: This assignment forced me to think in a much more practical and procedural way than many of my other projects. It is easy to talk about safety in broad terms, but it is much harder to build a plan that people could actually use in a high-pressure situation. One of the main issues I ran into was deciding how detailed the emergency action plan should be. My first instinct was to include as much information as possible because I wanted the plan to feel complete. As I worked through it, I realized that too much detail can make a plan harder to follow during an emergency, especially when people need clear direction quickly. I had to rethink parts of the document and focus more on clarity, chain of command, and communication flow. Another thing that stood out to me was how important role definition is in emergency planning. It is not enough to say staff should respond; there has to be a clear understanding of who does what, who communicates with whom, and how information moves under stress. This project changed how I think about safety planning because I no longer see it as just identifying risks. I see it as building a system that has to work in real time, under pressure, with real people carrying it out.

Sport Venue Management Coursework

Allegiant Stadium Booking Policy

Context: A structured booking policy outlining scheduling priorities, event tiers, and operational decision-making processes used to manage event calendars at a large multipurpose venue.

My Role: I developed the booking policy and supporting rationale independently.

Skills Demonstrated: Strategic planning, event prioritization, revenue-focused decision-making, policy development.

Reflection Draft: This assignment stood out to me because it required me to think less like a student completing a paper and more like a decision-maker who would actually have to defend a policy. Building the booking policy meant deciding how a venue should prioritize events when there are competing demands for space, staff time, and calendar availability. One of the hardest parts was that there was no perfect answer. I had to think about whether revenue-generating events should always come first or whether there are situations where smaller community-oriented events should receive priority because of the venue’s broader purpose or public value. I also had to consider the operational side of scheduling, including turnaround time between events, staffing limits, and the strain that frequent bookings can place on a facility. That pushed me to think more realistically about the difference between maximizing the calendar and managing it responsibly. What I took from this project is that strategic planning often comes down to making trade-offs and being able to justify them. A policy is not valuable just because it is organized on paper. It has to reflect the goals of the venue and also be realistic enough to work in practice.

Event Planning and Participant Experience

Event Operations / Risk Management Coursework

Event Risk Assessment

Context: An operational risk analysis of a large national touring concert event at Allegiant Stadium, identifying crowd, medical, and event-day hazards and proposing mitigation strategies.

My Role: I completed the risk analysis and developed the proposed mitigation measures.

Skills Demonstrated: Event risk assessment, hazard analysis, proactive planning, operational decision-making.

Reflection Draft: What made this assignment different from many others was that it required me to think ahead instead of reacting to a problem after it happens. I had to consider what could realistically go wrong during a major event and decide which risks deserved the most attention. Early in the process, I found myself identifying too many possible issues without really evaluating which ones mattered most. Over time, I realized that a good risk assessment is not just a long list of hazards. It requires prioritizing risks based on both likelihood and impact so that resources and planning efforts are directed where they are actually needed. That forced me to be more selective and more realistic. It also helped me understand that risk management is not a separate step that happens at the end of planning. It should shape decisions throughout the entire event process. This project changed how I think about planning because I now pay much more attention to what could disrupt operations, how those disruptions could affect people, and what can be done ahead of time to reduce that exposure.

Final Reflection Draft (Working Version)

Looking back across the work in this program, one of the biggest changes I see is in how I approach a problem. Earlier on, I was more focused on understanding concepts and making sure I could explain them clearly. As I moved through the program, I started thinking less about simply describing how an organization or facility works and more about what I would actually do if I were responsible for the outcome. That shift seems simple, but for me it changed the way I approached almost every major assignment.

A lot of that development came from assignments that required decisions rather than summaries. In projects like the booking policy, the emergency action plan, and the event risk assessment, I was not just repeating course concepts. I had to decide what mattered most, what trade-offs were acceptable, and how different choices could affect staff, guests, and the organization. That process helped me understand that management decisions are rarely as straightforward as they look from the outside. In many cases, there is no perfect answer. There is only a choice that has to be justified based on the context, the priorities of the organization, and the realities of implementation.

Another important area of growth for me was recognizing the difference between writing a document and creating something that could actually function in practice. Earlier in the program, I often thought that if a plan was detailed and complete, then it was strong. Over time, I started to see that usefulness matters more than length. A plan has to be clear, realistic, and usable by the people who would carry it out. That became especially obvious in the emergency planning and staff training work, where the value of the document depended on whether staff could realistically follow it in a high-pressure environment.

I also developed a stronger understanding of how deeply operations shape the guest experience. Before this program, I tended to think of guest experience mostly in visible terms, such as customer service, event atmosphere, or participant enjoyment. What I understand now is that many of the things guests value most are actually produced by systems they never see, including scheduling decisions, staffing preparation, communication procedures, and risk planning. In other words, a smooth guest experience is often the result of effective operational decisions made long before the guest arrives. That perspective has changed the way I think about both facilities and events because I now pay much more attention to the operational foundation underneath the public-facing experience.

Putting this portfolio together also made something else clear to me: completing work and presenting work are not the same thing. At first, I approached the portfolio too much like an archive of assignments. Through revising it, I started to better understand that a professional portfolio should explain not just what was completed, but how the work reflects growth, decision-making, and professional direction. That has probably been one of the most useful parts of this process because it forced me to step back and look for connections across the program instead of viewing each artifact as an isolated assignment.

Overall, this portfolio reflects more than just completed coursework. It reflects how my thinking has changed during the MPRTSM program, especially in learning how to evaluate operational problems, make more intentional decisions, and connect planning to real-world outcomes. I still see areas where I can continue improving, especially in continuing to sharpen how I present and frame my work professionally, but I also think this portfolio shows a clear progression from learning concepts in class to beginning to apply them in a more practical and professional way.
