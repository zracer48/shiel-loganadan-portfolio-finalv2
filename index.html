Professional Introduction

My name is Shiel Loganadan, and I am currently completing my Master’s in Parks, Recreation, Tourism and Sport Management (MPRTSM). I am interested in pursuing a career in sport and recreation facility operations and event management, particularly in environments where operations, safety, and guest experience all have to work together at a high level.

Coming into this program, I had a general understanding of how sport and recreation organizations function, but most of that understanding was from the outside looking in. I could usually tell when something seemed organized, efficient, or well run, but I did not fully understand how much planning and coordination it took behind the scenes to create that result. One of the biggest changes for me throughout the program has been learning to think less like an observer and more like someone who is actually responsible for those decisions.

Across my coursework, I was often put in situations where I had to do more than explain ideas from class. I had to make choices, defend those choices, and think through the consequences of different approaches. In assignments related to facility planning, booking policies, emergency action planning, and event risk, I had to decide what should be prioritized, what trade-offs were reasonable, and what a manager would need to consider in practice. Those choices were not always simple. In many cases, I had to weigh efficiency against safety, revenue against access, or detail against usability.

One of the biggest takeaways from this program has been realizing that a strong guest experience is often built by decisions the guest never sees directly: planning, staffing, communication, preparation, and risk management.

SEVM Facility Planning Project

Facility Planning Project

Featured artifact in venue analysis and operational thinking

Context: A comprehensive analysis of Allegiant Stadium examining venue design, operational considerations, and the strategic role of a multipurpose stadium in a tourism-driven market.

My Role: I independently researched, analyzed, and developed the written facility planning project.

Skills Demonstrated: Facility planning, venue operations analysis, tourism market understanding, strategic facility evaluation.

Reflection: When I first started this project, I approached it mostly as a research assignment and focused on gathering information about Allegiant Stadium. As I worked further into it, I realized that simply describing the venue was not enough. I had to start asking why certain design and operational decisions were made and what impact those choices would actually have on the way the stadium functions. One of the biggest challenges was connecting physical features to operational outcomes. For example, I initially saw entry points, seating layout, and circulation areas as design details, but through the project I started viewing them as factors that directly influence crowd flow, staffing needs, guest access, and even emergency response.

Facility Emergency Action Plan

Emergency response structure and venue safety planning

Context: Development of an emergency response framework outlining staff roles, communication procedures, evacuation planning, and safety coordination in a large stadium environment.

My Role: I created the emergency action plan based on best practices and course guidance.

Skills Demonstrated: Risk management, emergency preparedness, communication planning, venue safety protocol design.

Reflection: This assignment forced me to think in a much more practical and procedural way than many of my other projects. It is easy to talk about safety in broad terms, but it is much harder to build a plan that people could actually use in a high-pressure situation. One of the main issues I ran into was deciding how detailed the emergency action plan should be. My first instinct was to include as much information as possible because I wanted the plan to feel complete. As I worked through it, I realized that too much detail can make a plan harder to follow during an emergency.

Final Reflection

Looking back across the work in this program, one of the biggest changes I see is in how I approach a problem. Earlier on, I was more focused on understanding concepts and making sure I could explain them clearly. As I moved through the program, I started thinking less about simply describing how an organization or facility works and more about what I would actually do if I were responsible for the outcome. That shift seems simple, but for me it changed the way I approached almost every major assignment.

A lot of that development came from assignments that required decisions rather than summaries. In projects like the booking policy, the emergency action plan, and the event risk assessment, I was not just repeating course concepts. I had to decide what mattered most, what trade-offs were acceptable, and how different choices could affect staff, guests, and the organization. That process helped me understand that management decisions are rarely as straightforward as they look from the outside.

I also developed a stronger understanding of how deeply operations shape the guest experience. Before this program, I tended to think of guest experience mostly in visible terms, such as customer service, event atmosphere, or participant enjoyment. What I understand now is that many of the things guests value most are actually produced by systems they never see, including scheduling decisions, staffing preparation, communication procedures, and risk planning.

Putting this portfolio together also made something else clear to me: completing work and presenting work are not the same thing. At first, I approached the portfolio too much like an archive of assignments. Through revising it, I started to better understand that a professional portfolio should explain not just what was completed, but how the work reflects growth, decision-making, and professional direction.
