Professional Introduction

My name is Shiel Loganadan, and I am completing my Master’s in Parks, Recreation, Tourism and Sport Management (MPRTSM). I am interested in pursuing a career in sport and recreation facility operations, event management, and planning roles where safety, logistics, participant experience, and organizational priorities all have to work together.

One of the biggest changes in my thinking during this program has been learning to move beyond description and toward judgment. Earlier on, I could explain concepts and identify issues, but I was not yet consistently approaching assignments the way a manager or planner would. Over time, I became much more focused on what should be prioritized, what trade-offs were realistic, and how a recommendation would actually function in a live setting.

That shift became especially clear in projects involving facility planning, benchmarking, public engagement, event design, tourism development, and organizational diagnosis. In those assignments, I had to interpret evidence, decide what mattered most, and communicate recommendations in a way that another person could actually use. The strongest lesson I took from that work is that professional readiness is not just about knowing information. It is about selecting the right information, framing it clearly, and connecting it to action.

I also came to understand participant and guest experience differently. At the start of the program, I tended to think about experience in visible terms such as atmosphere, enjoyment, or service. What I understand now is that strong experiences are often produced by systems people barely notice, including staffing, sequencing, access, communication, timing, and risk planning. That has changed the way I approach both operational and public-facing work.

The clearest lesson I took from this program is that strong participant and guest experiences are usually built long before the public arrives through planning, sequencing, communication, access, and operational follow-through.

That forced me to think more like a practitioner working within limits and helped me better understand how access, scheduling, and facility planning interact in real systems.

Final Paper – City of Naples Parks & Recreation Department

Diagnosing and Reframing a City Parks & Recreation Department

Organizational diagnosis, leadership frames, and strategic redesign
Why this matters: Demonstrates my ability to analyze organizational problems from multiple perspectives rather than jumping to a single-cause explanation.

Context: This paper analyzed the City of Naples Parks & Recreation Department through organizational behavior and leadership frameworks, focusing on strategy-execution gaps and mission drift.

Skills Demonstrated: Organizational analysis, leadership framing, strategic diagnosis, synthesis, and policy-oriented writing.

Reflection: This project helped me see how easy it is to misread an organizational problem if it is viewed through only one lens. Using multiple frames showed me how structure, culture, politics, and human factors interact. The most important lesson for me was that visible operational problems often reflect deeper alignment issues. That made me more careful about diagnosing complex systems and more intentional about building recommendations that address underlying causes rather than surface symptoms alone.

Final Reflection

Looking across this portfolio, the most important change I see is not just that my writing improved. It is that my thinking became more selective, more implementation-oriented, and more grounded in decisions rather than description. Earlier in the program, I was more focused on understanding concepts and presenting complete work. Over time, I became much more focused on what should be prioritized, what trade-offs were realistic, and how a plan or recommendation would actually function in practice.

A clear pattern across these artifacts is that I increasingly approached assignments as if someone would need to use them, not just grade them. That meant paying closer attention to clarity, sequencing, evidence selection, and the constraints that shape real organizations. Whether I was working on facility planning, benchmarking, organizational diagnosis, event design, or destination analysis, the strongest growth came from having to make judgments rather than simply summarize information.

I also developed a much stronger understanding of the relationship between operations and experience. What participants, guests, or residents experience on the surface is often shaped by systems they do not see directly, including staffing, access, timing, communication, preparation, and risk planning. That realization changed how I look at both facilities and events because I now pay much more attention to the behind-the-scenes choices that support visible outcomes.

Another major area of growth was learning how to present work as a professional narrative rather than a collection of completed assignments. Revising this portfolio made it clear that strong presentation requires selectivity. The work included here was chosen because it most directly demonstrates the competencies, habits of thinking, and types of judgment I want employers to see. In that sense, the portfolio reflects not only what I completed in the program, but how I now understand my own strengths more clearly.

Overall, this portfolio shows a progression from learning concepts in class to applying them in a more practical, analytical, and professionally focused way. I still see areas where I can continue improving, but I also think this work shows that I am better prepared to contribute in settings where planning, operations, communication, and participant experience all need to work together.

What this portfolio represents:

More than anything, this portfolio represents a shift from completing academic work to making clearer decisions, asking better questions, justifying trade-offs, and presenting information in a form that supports real use.
